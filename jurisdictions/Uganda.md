## Executive Summary

Uganda currently has limited formal regulation specifically for cryptocurrency. The Bank of Uganda has generally maintained a cautious position, and there is no dedicated licensing framework for virtual asset service providers as of mid-2026.

Bitcoin is not legal tender. Self-custody is not prohibited, but the lack of clear rules creates uncertainty for businesses.

---

## 1. Regulatory Overview

- **No dedicated crypto-specific law** currently in force.
- **Bank of Uganda** has issued cautions regarding virtual currencies.
- **Self-Custody**: Not prohibited for individuals.
- **Business Activities**: Operate without a clear licensing or regulatory framework.

---

## 2. Taxation

Specific tax treatment for cryptocurrency remains limited. General tax principles would apply to any gains or income derived from virtual assets.

---

## 3. Key Developments (2025–2026)

- Limited formal progress on cryptocurrency-specific regulation.
- Continued central bank caution.
- Broader discussions on fintech and digital assets ongoing, but crypto remains largely unregulated at the specific level.

---

## 4. Key Players

- Bank of Uganda
- Uganda Revenue Authority
- Capital Markets Authority

---

## 5. Philosophical & Legal Alignment

The current environment permits individual self-custody in practice but lacks clear legal recognition or regulatory infrastructure. This creates both opportunities (freedom for individuals) and risks (uncertainty for businesses). The situation is less aligned with the framework’s preference for clear legal treatment compared to jurisdictions with developed frameworks.

---

## 6. Adoption Implications

Uganda has shown interest in cryptocurrency consistent with regional trends. However, the absence of clear regulation limits institutional participation and creates risks for businesses. The jurisdiction illustrates both the potential and the challenges of operating in a lightly regulated environment.
