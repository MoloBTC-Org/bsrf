## Executive Summary

On 30 June / 1 July 2026, Taiwan’s Legislative Yuan passed the **Virtual Asset Service Act** (虛擬資產服務法) on third reading. This represents Taiwan’s first comprehensive dedicated legislation for virtual assets.

The law marks a significant shift from the previous anti-money laundering (AML) registration regime to a full **licensing regime** for Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs). It also introduces Taiwan’s first legal framework for stablecoins.

While the new law increases regulatory oversight on intermediaries, self-custodied Bitcoin remains outside its direct scope.

---

## 1. Historical and Current Regulatory Context

Prior to this law, Taiwan regulated virtual assets primarily through:

- AML registration requirements for VASPs (under the Money Laundering Control Act).
- Treatment of cryptocurrencies as “virtual commodities” rather than currency or securities by the Financial Supervisory Commission (FSC) and Central Bank.
- Existing consumer protection and fraud prevention laws.

This created a relatively light-touch environment compared to many jurisdictions, but lacked a dedicated legal framework for market conduct, custody standards, and stablecoins.

---

## 2. The Virtual Asset Service Act (2026)

### Key Provisions

- **Licensing Regime**: All VASPs (including exchanges, custodians, and certain service providers) must obtain a license from the FSC before operating. Operating without a license is a criminal offence.
- **Transition Period**: Existing AML-registered platforms have 12 months to apply for a license and up to 21 months to obtain approval.
- **Stablecoins**: Establishes a framework for stablecoin issuance. Issuers must maintain 100% reserves, with assets held in trust by domestic financial institutions. Approval from both the FSC and Central Bank is required.
- **Operational Requirements**: VASPs must implement robust internal controls, cybersecurity measures, business continuity plans, and proper segregation of customer assets.
- **Penalties**: Significant criminal and administrative penalties for unlicensed operation (up to 7 years imprisonment and fines up to NT$100 million) and for market manipulation or fraud (3–10 years imprisonment).

## 3. Implications for Bitcoin and Self-Custody

- **Self-Custody**: The law primarily targets intermediaries (VASPs). Self-custodied Bitcoin held by individuals remains outside the direct licensing and operational requirements.
- **Bitcoin vs Stablecoins**: Bitcoin itself is not classified as a stablecoin under the new framework. The law’s stablecoin provisions are more relevant to fiat-pegged tokens.
- **Sui Generis Alignment**: Taiwan’s approach of creating a dedicated law for virtual assets (rather than forcing them into existing securities or banking laws) is broadly consistent with recognising the distinct nature of decentralised assets like Bitcoin.

---

## 4. Adoption and Strategic Implications

Taiwan’s new law brings greater regulatory clarity and investor protection but also increases compliance costs for platforms. For self-custodial users and Bitcoin maximalists, the law reinforces the importance of staying outside custodial and intermediary structures where possible.

The combination of licensing, reserve requirements for stablecoins, and strong penalties signals Taiwan’s intent to balance innovation with robust safeguards.

**Truth & Clarity Summary**  
Taiwan has joined the growing number of jurisdictions moving from light-touch AML registration to a comprehensive licensing regime for virtual assets. The Virtual Asset Service Act represents a material development in Asia’s regulatory landscape. Self-custody of Bitcoin remains a viable and lower-friction option under the new framework.
