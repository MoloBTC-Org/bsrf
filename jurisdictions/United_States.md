## Executive Summary

The United States maintains a fragmented but relatively permissive environment for Bitcoin compared to many jurisdictions. Self-custody is generally respected, and taxation applies on a realisation basis. Bitcoin is treated as property by the IRS.

A significant recent development occurred on 22–23 June 2026, when the Senate passed the **21st Century ROAD to Housing Act**, which includes a temporary prohibition on the Federal Reserve issuing a retail CBDC until 31 December 2030. This represents meaningful congressional pushback against a potential surveillance-oriented central bank digital currency.

---

## 1. Historical Thread

Bitcoin has been present in U.S. regulatory discussions since at least 2013. Key developments include:

- FinCEN guidance treating Bitcoin exchangers as money services businesses.
- IRS Notice 2014-21 treating virtual currency as property.
- SEC and CFTC enforcement actions on token classification.
- Ongoing legislative efforts (e.g., FIT21, CLARITY Act proposals).

---

## 2. Current Regulatory Overview

### CBDC Development (June 2026)
On 22 June 2026, the US Senate passed the **21st Century ROAD to Housing Act** (85-5 vote). Embedded within the bill is a provision that prohibits the Federal Reserve from issuing, establishing, or promoting a retail central bank digital currency until **31 December 2030**.

Key points:
- The ban is **temporary** (approximately four years).
- It applies to a **retail CBDC**.
- Stablecoins issued by private entities are **not** covered by this prohibition.
- The bill must still pass the House and be signed by the President. It is widely expected to advance.

This development reflects strong bipartisan concern regarding privacy, surveillance, and the expansion of central bank power.

### Taxation
Bitcoin is treated as property. Gains are taxed on realisation (sale or exchange). There is currently no federal wealth tax or unrealised gains tax on crypto holdings.

### Self-Custody
Generally protected. No broad federal requirement exists to report self-custodied Bitcoin holdings (though foreign account reporting rules such as FBAR may apply in specific cases).

### Securities vs Commodities
Bitcoin itself is widely regarded as a commodity rather than a security.

## 5. Philosophical & Natural-Law Synthesis

The U.S. has strong constitutional protections for property (5th and 14th Amendments) and privacy. The sui generis nature of self-custodied Bitcoin aligns well with these protections. The mind-resident control of Bitcoin engages both property rights and potential First Amendment considerations regarding compelled speech.

The recent Senate action on CBDCs reinforces the framework’s core distinction between decentralised, self-custodial money (Bitcoin) and centrally issued digital currency designed for surveillance and control.

---

## 6. Adoption Implications

The temporary CBDC ban reduces near-term regulatory risk for Bitcoin holders and self-custody advocates in the United States. It creates breathing room for Bitcoin infrastructure development while Congress continues to debate broader crypto legislation.

Long-term advocacy should focus on making permanent restrictions on retail CBDCs and codifying strong protections for self-custodied Bitcoin.
