## Purpose

This document serves as a living compendium of significant court rulings and enforcement actions involving Bitcoin and crypto assets. It focuses particularly on matters relevant to self-custody, property rights, taxation, regulatory classification, and the limits of state enforcement against decentralised monetary instruments.

The compendium prioritises cases and actions that have been referenced within the Bitcoin Sovereignty Research Framework and related work.

## South Africa

### Standard Bank of South Africa v South African Reserve Bank [2025] ZAGPPHC 481 (15 May 2025)

**Court:** High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria (Motha J)  
**Key Holding:** Cryptocurrency does not constitute “money” or “capital” within the meaning of the Exchange Control Regulations, 1961.  
**Reasoning:** The Court emphasised the decentralised and intangible nature of cryptocurrency and held that it fell outside the regulatory definitions applicable to traditional money and capital.  
**Status:** Remains good law until overturned on appeal by a higher court.  
**Relevance to Framework:** Directly supports the position that Bitcoin cannot be straightforwardly classified under existing exchange control categories designed for intermediary-dependent financial instruments.

### Mangundhla and Another v South African Reserve Bank (1 June 2026)

**Court:** High Court of South Africa  
**Key Holding:** Bitcoin was held to constitute both “money” and “capital” for the purposes of exchange control regulation in the specific factual context before the Court.  
**Reasoning:** The Court explicitly departed from the *Standard Bank* decision, describing it as “clearly wrong”.  
**Status:** Significant for the development of the Draft Capital Flow Management Regulations 2026.  
**Relevance to Framework:** Creates direct tension with the *Standard Bank* precedent and has been cited as a catalyst for the current regulatory push to bring Bitcoin within capital flow controls.

## United States

### General Position on Classification

US courts and regulators have addressed Bitcoin and crypto assets across multiple categories (money, property, commodity, security). Key themes include:

- **IRS Notice 2014-21**: Virtual currency is treated as property for federal tax purposes. Gains or losses are calculated on realisation.
- **SEC Enforcement Actions**: The SEC has pursued enforcement on the basis that certain tokens constitute securities. Bitcoin itself has generally been treated as a commodity rather than a security in enforcement and regulatory commentary.
- **Commodity Classification**: The CFTC has asserted jurisdiction over Bitcoin as a commodity.

**Notable Case References in Framework Context:**
- SEC v. Ripple Labs, Inc. and others (ongoing and related proceedings) — litigation concerning whether XRP constitutes a security.
- Various district court and tax court decisions addressing the tax treatment of cryptocurrency as property.

**Relevance to Framework:** The fragmented US approach (different regulators asserting jurisdiction over different aspects) illustrates both the difficulty of forcing Bitcoin into pre-existing legal categories and the relative strength of self-custody in avoiding intermediary-dependent enforcement surfaces.

## United Kingdom

UK courts have recognised cryptoassets as property for certain legal purposes.

**Key Themes from Case Law:**
- Cryptoassets have been held to constitute property capable of being the subject of proprietary claims, tracing, and remedies in insolvency.
- Common law development has proceeded on a case-by-case basis, with courts applying traditional property principles to novel digital assets.

**Relevance to Framework:** The UK approach demonstrates that common law systems can extend property protections to Bitcoin without requiring legislative reclassification as “money” or “capital”. This aligns with the sui generis bearer property analysis.

## European Union

### MiCA Implementation and Related Litigation

The Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) has generated a growing body of national-level implementation and enforcement activity across EU Member States.

**Key Issues Emerging:**
- Licensing and conduct requirements for Crypto-Asset Service Providers (CASPs).
- Classification of different types of crypto assets.
- Interaction between MiCA and existing financial services regulation.

**Relevance to Framework:** MiCA represents a comprehensive attempt to regulate intermediary activities in the crypto space. It does not directly regulate self-custody or the Bitcoin protocol itself, reinforcing the distinction between on-chain activity and regulated service provision.

## Enforcement Actions (Non-Judicial but Significant)

While not court judgments, the following enforcement actions have been referenced in the Framework as illustrative of the limits and reach of state power:

- **OFAC Actions against Tether (USDT) (2025–2026)**: Freezing of substantial USDT holdings, demonstrating the vulnerability of issuer-controlled stablecoins to unilateral state action.
- **FINRA Action against Blue Ocean ATS, LLC (May 2026)**: Significant fine for AML and supervisory failures in a high-volume trading venue.

These actions highlight the contrast between assets held through centralised intermediaries (vulnerable to rapid freeze or enforcement) and properly self-custodied Bitcoin (resistant to such measures without direct action against the individual holder).
