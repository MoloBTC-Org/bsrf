## Executive Summary

The Bitcoin Sovereignty Research Framework exists to establish, articulate, and defend a coherent legal, philosophical, and operational understanding of Bitcoin as a form of **sui generis bearer property** and an **adjacent currency** to state-issued money.

This framework rejects the common tendency to force Bitcoin into pre-existing legal categories (currency, capital, commodity, or security) that were never designed for a decentralised, mind-resident, energy-secured monetary system. Instead, it advances a position grounded in natural law principles of property, privacy, and self-ownership, while remaining practically useful for policy engagement, legal advocacy, and sovereign practice.

The framework is organised into two interconnected tiers:

- **Tier 1 – Foundations**: Establishes the jurisprudential, philosophical, and historical basis for treating Bitcoin as sui generis bearer property and adjacent currency.
- **Tier 2 – Operational**: Translates these foundations into practical guidance on risk, infrastructure, implementation, and self-custody at scale.

This document serves as the central reference point for the entire repository. All nation-specific analyses, supporting legal research, and operational tools are intended to be read in light of the principles articulated here.

## 2. Core Thesis

Bitcoin is best understood as **sui generis bearer property** acquired through homesteading via proof-of-work energy expenditure. Control over Bitcoin can reside entirely in the mind through the memorisation or secure offline storage of a seed phrase. This characteristic fundamentally distinguishes it from all other forms of money and financial assets.

As a result, Bitcoin functions as an **adjacent currency** — a decentralised monetary instrument that operates alongside state-issued currencies without requiring legal tender status or central issuance.

This position carries several direct implications:

- Mere holding of self-custodied Bitcoin should not constitute a taxable event.
- Taxation should be limited to realised gains arising from voluntary economic activity with a sufficient territorial nexus.
- Compelled disclosure of private keys or seed phrases engages fundamental protections against compelled speech and invasion of mental privacy.
- Regulatory frameworks that treat Bitcoin identically to issuer-dependent crypto assets are conceptually flawed and practically unenforceable.

## 4. Tier 1 – Foundations (Detailed)

### 4.1 Sui Generis Bearer Property

Bitcoin satisfies the classical requirements of property through homesteading. The expenditure of real energy in proof-of-work mining constitutes the mixing of labour with unowned resources. Once acquired, control is exercised through cryptographic keys that can exist solely in the mind.

This creates a category of property that is:
- Portable across jurisdictions without physical movement
- Resistant to physical seizure without compelled disclosure
- Fundamentally private by default

These characteristics engage deep constitutional protections in virtually every jurisdiction regarding property, privacy, and freedom from compelled speech.

### 4.2 Adjacent Currency Status

Bitcoin was designed and functions as peer-to-peer electronic cash. It performs the core monetary functions of medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account without requiring state recognition or central issuance.

Treating Bitcoin as merely another financial asset or investment vehicle ignores its primary monetary purpose. The adjacent currency model provides a more coherent framework for both taxation and regulation.

### 4.3 Distinction from Other Crypto Assets

Bitcoin’s lack of issuer, fixed supply, and decentralised consensus mechanism distinguish it fundamentally from the majority of other crypto assets, which depend on identifiable promoters, foundations, or companies.

This distinction is not merely technical — it has direct legal and policy consequences. Regulatory frameworks that apply identical treatment to Bitcoin and issuer-dependent tokens are intellectually incoherent.

### 4.4 Right to Sovereign Private Compute

The ability to run open-source software, including Bitcoin nodes and related tools, on personal devices without interference or compelled disclosure constitutes a fundamental extension of the rights to privacy, property, and freedom of expression. This right — referred to as the Right to Sovereign Private Compute — must be protected as digital identity systems expand globally (including developments under EU eIDAS frameworks, UK digital identity initiatives, and equivalent programmes in other jurisdictions). Any regulatory framework that undermines the capacity of individuals to operate private, sovereign compute environments directly conflicts with these foundational rights.
