# United States Nation Document  
**Bitcoin Regulation, Self-Custody & Sui Generis Treatment**  
*(Created 23 June 2026)*

**Executive Summary**  
The United States has a complex and fragmented regulatory landscape for Bitcoin, involving multiple federal agencies (SEC, CFTC, IRS, FinCEN) and state regulators. While self-custody is generally respected, regulatory clarity remains limited, particularly around classification of Bitcoin and enforcement priorities.

Taxation is generally on a realisation basis, with Bitcoin treated as property by the IRS. There is no federal wealth tax or unrealised gains tax on crypto at present.

The sui generis framework developed in this project is particularly relevant in the U.S. context due to ongoing debates around Bitcoin’s classification and the strong constitutional protections for property and privacy.

**1. Historical Thread**  
Bitcoin has been present in U.S. regulatory discussions since at least 2013. Key developments include:
- FinCEN guidance treating Bitcoin exchangers as money services businesses.
- IRS Notice 2014-21 treating virtual currency as property.
- SEC and CFTC enforcement actions attempting to classify various tokens as securities or commodities.
- Ongoing legislative efforts (e.g., FIT21, CLARITY Act proposals).

**2. Current Regulatory Overview**  
- **Federal Level**: No comprehensive federal crypto law. Regulation occurs through existing statutes interpreted by multiple agencies.
- **Taxation**: Bitcoin is treated as property. Gains are taxed on realisation (sale or exchange). No federal unrealised gains or wealth tax on crypto.
- **Self-custody**: Generally protected. No broad federal requirement to report self-custodied holdings (though reporting obligations exist for certain foreign accounts).
- **Securities/Commodities**: Bitcoin itself is widely viewed as a commodity rather than a security.

**3. Key Players**

**5. Philosophical & Natural-Law Synthesis**

The U.S. has strong constitutional protections for property (5th and 14th Amendments) and privacy. The sui generis nature of self-custodied Bitcoin aligns well with these protections. The mind-resident control of Bitcoin engages both property rights and potential First Amendment considerations (compelled speech via seed phrase disclosure).

The U.S. remains one of the most important jurisdictions for Bitcoin development and adoption. Clearer recognition of Bitcoin’s sui generis status would reduce regulatory uncertainty and support innovation while respecting constitutional rights.
