## Purpose

This document sets out the core principles of the Bitcoin Sovereignty Research Framework. These principles provide the authoritative foundation for all work within the framework, including nation-specific analyses, policy submissions, and operational guidance.

They are intended to be clear, quotable, and consistently applied. The detailed reasoning and analysis supporting these principles is contained in the **Global Framework Document**.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Bitcoin as Sui Generis Bearer Property**  
Bitcoin is justly acquired property through the process of homesteading via proof-of-work energy expenditure. Control can reside entirely in the mind through private keys or seed phrases. This creates a unique category of property that engages fundamental protections regarding privacy, property, and freedom from compelled speech.

**2. Bitcoin as Adjacent Currency**  
Bitcoin functions as a decentralised monetary instrument that operates alongside state-issued currencies. It performs the core monetary roles of medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account without requiring central issuance or legal tender status.

**3. Right to Sovereign Private Compute**  
The right to operate open-source software, including Bitcoin nodes and related tools, on personal devices without interference or compelled disclosure is a fundamental extension of the rights to privacy, property, and freedom of expression. This right must be protected against expanding digital identity systems and surveillance infrastructure.

**4. Self-Custody as the Highest Standard**  
Self-custody, or decentralised multi-party control, represents the highest and most consistent expression of control over Bitcoin. It should be recognised and protected as the preferred standard for both natural and juristic persons.

**5. Distinction Between Bitcoin and Issuer-Dependent Crypto Assets**  
Bitcoin’s lack of issuer, fixed supply, and decentralised consensus distinguish it fundamentally from crypto assets that depend on identifiable promoters or central entities. Regulatory and tax frameworks should maintain this distinction.

**6. Realisation-Based Taxation with Territorial Nexus**  
Taxation of Bitcoin should apply only to realised gains arising from voluntary economic activity with a sufficient territorial nexus to the relevant jurisdiction. Mere holding or cross-border movement of self-custodied Bitcoin should not constitute a taxable event.

**7. Protection Against Compelled Disclosure**  
Compelled disclosure of private keys or seed phrases engages fundamental protections against compelled speech and invasion of mental privacy. Such compulsion should be subject to the highest level of scrutiny.

**8. Temporary Protections During Regulatory Uncertainty**  
Where regulations are unclear, conflicting, or in transition, jurisdictions should implement temporary protections, including amnesty windows, extended grace periods, and safe harbours for actors acting in good faith.

**9. Right to Noding and Open Monetary Infrastructure**  
The operation of Bitcoin nodes and participation in decentralised monetary infrastructure constitutes a legitimate exercise of open-source rights and should be protected as such.
