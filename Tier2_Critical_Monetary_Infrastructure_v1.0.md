## Executive Summary

This document treats Bitcoin self-custody as **critical monetary infrastructure** for individuals and organisations. It focuses on the practical requirements for building, operating, and maintaining sovereign monetary systems that are resilient, auditable, and aligned with the philosophical and risk principles established in the other Tier 2 documents.

The emphasis is on **training, tooling, implementation pathways, and real-world validation** — moving from strategy into operational reality.

---

## 1. Introduction: Self-Custody as Infrastructure

Bitcoin self-custody is not merely a personal security practice. When adopted at scale by individuals, families, organisations, and communities, it becomes **critical monetary infrastructure** — the foundational layer that enables economic sovereignty, resilience against institutional failure, and permissionless participation in the global economy.

Treating self-custody as infrastructure requires the same rigour applied to other critical systems: documented processes, trained personnel, tested procedures, appropriate tooling, and continuous improvement.

---

## 2. Core Competency Areas

Organisations and individuals serious about self-custody should develop capability across the following domains:

### 2.1 Key Management & Recovery
- Secure generation, storage, and rotation of seed material
- Multi-signature architectures and threshold schemes
- Tested recovery procedures (including scenarios for lost keys, compromised devices, or personnel changes)
- Inheritance and succession planning

### 2.2 Operational Security (OpSec)
- Air-gapped workflows and signing environments
- Physical security of signing devices and backup materials
- Information security hygiene (avoiding metadata leakage and using secure communication channels)
- Insider threat mitigation

### 2.3 Tooling & Technical Architecture
- Recommended hardware (signing devices, air-gapped computers, secure storage)
- Software stack (wallets, node software, verification tools)
- Integration with existing treasury or accounting systems where appropriate
- Backup and redundancy strategies

### 2.4 Governance & Process
- Clear roles and responsibilities (key ceremonies, spending approvals, audits)
- Documented procedures and runbooks
- Regular testing and tabletop exercises
- Audit trails without compromising operational security

---

## 3. Implementation Pathways

This document outlines progressive pathways suitable for different scales:

- **Individual / Family**: Focus on robust personal opsec, multisig where appropriate, and documented recovery plans.
- **Small Organisation / Treasury**: Introduce formal procedures, role separation, and basic multisig governance.
- **Larger Treasury / Institutional**: Full defence-in-depth architecture, regular audits, geographic distribution, and integration with broader risk management frameworks.

Each pathway builds on the previous one, allowing organisations to mature their capabilities over time.

---

## 4. Training & Human Factors

Technical controls are only as strong as the people who operate them. This document emphasises:

- Structured training for anyone with access to keys or signing authority
- Clear documentation that is actually used (not shelfware)
- Culture of security without paranoia
- Regular review and updating of procedures as the threat landscape and tooling evolve
