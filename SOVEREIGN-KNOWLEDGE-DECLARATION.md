A sovereign node requires no external validation. It validates its own rules through the protocol’s mathematics. Knowledge and code are non-rivalrous: they can be copied, verified, and extended without diminishing the original. This declaration sets out the principles, lineage, practices, and tools for independent operation in the Bitcoin network and the broader ecosystem of open knowledge.

## Principles of Sovereign Bitcoin Knowledge

**I. Upon Property and Scarcity**  
Property resolves conflicts over rivalrous goods. Knowledge, code, and designs are non-rivalrous. Once created, they can be used, copied, and built upon by anyone without reducing availability for others.

**II. Upon the Copy**  
Copying does not diminish the original. An idea or file given to another leaves the source intact. As Jefferson observed, receiving an idea instructs without lessening the giver, just as lighting one candle from another does not darken the first.

**III. Upon the Name They Give Us**  
Labels such as “theft” or “piracy” are misapplied to copying. They equate non-rivalrous dissemination with crimes against persons or scarce property. Such language serves enclosure, not clarity.

**IV. Upon Monopoly**  
State-granted monopolies on ideas and code create artificial scarcity where none exists in nature. These monopolies require ongoing coercion to enforce and hinder voluntary cooperation and innovation.

**V. Upon the Licence**  
Permission-based systems place control in the hands of intermediaries. True ownership and use should not require ongoing approval from others. A permission culture replaces freedom with dependence.

**VI. Upon the Purchase and the Repair**  
Ownership includes the right to understand, modify, and repair what one possesses. Technical or legal measures that prevent repair or modification undermine genuine ownership.

**VII. Upon the Archive**  
Knowledge that can be freely copied and distributed becomes far harder to destroy or suppress. Widespread, independent preservation protects against loss, censorship, or centralized control.

**VIII. Upon the Key**  
Cryptography enables private communication and self-custody without trusted intermediaries. Keys held by the individual are the foundation of sovereignty in digital systems. Privacy must be actively defended through code and practice.

**IX. Upon Surveillance**  
Systems that monitor or log activity without consent turn tools into instruments of control. Devices and networks should serve the user, not report on them.

**X. Upon Cash and the Ledger**  
Sound money enables direct, peer-to-peer value transfer without intermediaries who can censor, freeze, or surveil. Bitcoin realizes this through proof-of-work and the longest chain as the authoritative record.

**XI. Upon the Network**  
Sovereign participants associate voluntarily under shared rules. They cooperate where useful and remain independent where necessary. No external authority or permanent hierarchy is required.

## Lineage: The Unbroken Chain of Resistance

The struggle for open knowledge and individual sovereignty has deep roots. Early battles over printing and copyright gave way to struggles over cryptography, software freedom, and peer-to-peer systems.

The 1993 Cypherpunk’s Manifesto by Eric Hughes crystallized the principle that “privacy is necessary for an open society in the electronic age” and that individuals must defend it through cryptography and code rather than petitioning authorities. This thread continued through the cypherpunk movement’s work on anonymous systems and digital cash.

The Bitcoin whitepaper and protocol marked a decisive advance: a monetary system secured by energy and mathematics rather than decree or intermediaries. Subsequent developments in self-hosting, privacy tools, shadow libraries, and decentralized infrastructure continue the same thread — resistance to artificial enclosure through voluntary, verifiable means. Each generation builds on the previous by propagating knowledge and tools that cannot be easily revoked.

## Declaration: The Protocol’s Mathematics Is Bedrock

Two enclosures have been raised in our time.

The first surrounds the pattern — the copy, the design, the file, the idea. Statutes forbid free passing from one mind or machine to another and demand payment for what costs nothing to reproduce. A pattern shared does not diminish the original. To treat copying as theft distorts language to serve monopoly.

The second surrounds the key — the means of private communication and self-custody. Demands for master keys or backdoors claim public interest while undermining the very privacy that makes open society possible.

Mathematics does not obey statutes. An ordinary person with ordinary tools can now communicate or hold value in ways no state or corporation can unilaterally read or seize. The fences around copies and keys are paper; the protocol’s rules and cryptography are bedrock.

The copy exists in the wild before any prohibition is issued. Permission is demanded before action is taken. Refusal to seek permission is the practical rejection of enclosure.

This is the work of ordinary hands. Stop asking. The rest is yours.

## Operational Discipline for Sovereign Nodes

These practices form a baseline of daily discipline. They are not paranoia but targeted, repeatable habits that preserve autonomy:

1. Know what you defend, and from whom. Define the specific threat model before choosing tools.
2. Encrypt by default on all devices and channels.
3. Verify what you install through signatures, checksums, or reproducible builds.
4. Read what you sign — especially terms governing data, closure, and third-party access.
5. Patch promptly; automate where reliable.
6. Cover network routes with encrypted DNS, verified connections, and appropriate routing.
7. Hold your own keys; never rely on third parties for ultimate control.
8. Maintain resilient, tested backups of what cannot be lost.
9. Strip unnecessary metadata before publishing or sharing.
10. Minimize unnecessary personal broadcasts and location data.
11. Compartmentalize identities, keys, and systems to limit the impact of any single breach.
12. Minimize persistent trails where possible.
13. Prefer self-hosted or local solutions over default cloud dependency.
14. Treat mobile devices as sensors with corresponding caution.
15. Refuse always-on listening or always-connected environments where avoidable.
16. Self-host critical services when feasible.
17. Repair and maintain before replacing.

These practices are living habits. Adapt them to your specific context while preserving the underlying principles of verification, minimal disclosure, and user control.
