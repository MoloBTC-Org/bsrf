## Executive Summary

This whitepaper argues that self-custodied Bitcoin is best understood as an **adjacent currency** to state-issued money under principles of international law and monetary theory. It proposes a coherent framework for taxation and regulation that respects Bitcoin’s unique technological and economic characteristics while remaining compatible with existing legal systems.

## 1. Bitcoin’s Monetary Function

Bitcoin was explicitly designed as peer-to-peer electronic cash. It functions as:
- A medium of exchange
- A store of value
- A unit of account (in growing adoption)

Its fixed supply and decentralised nature make it function as a form of **hard money** in the Austrian economic tradition.

## 2. The Concept of Adjacent Currency

An adjacent currency is a monetary instrument that:
- Operates alongside state-issued currencies
- Is not issued or controlled by any state
- Serves monetary functions without requiring legal tender status

Historical examples include:
- Gold and silver coins alongside fiat currencies
- Foreign currencies in dollarized economies
- Community currencies and local exchange systems

Bitcoin fits naturally into this category.

## 3. International Law Considerations

Under principles of monetary sovereignty and international economic law:
- States have the right to issue their own currency
- States do not have an unlimited right to restrict the use of alternative monetary instruments by their citizens, especially when those instruments are decentralised and borderless
- The right to property and freedom of contract support the use of Bitcoin as a medium of exchange

Attempts to treat Bitcoin as equivalent to traditional capital or investment assets ignore its primary monetary function.

## 4. Tax Implications of Adjacent Currency Status

If Bitcoin is recognised as an adjacent currency, the logical tax treatment is:

- **Holding**: Not a taxable event (similar to holding foreign currency cash)
- **Realised gains**: Taxed only when Bitcoin is exchanged for goods, services, or state currency with a clear territorial connection
- **Cross-border movement**: Should not trigger capital controls when the Bitcoin remains self-custodied

This approach avoids the incoherence of trying to tax unrealised gains on a decentralised, mind-resident asset.

## 5. Regulatory Implications

Treating Bitcoin as an adjacent currency supports:
- Clear separation between Bitcoin and issuer-dependent crypto assets
- Protection of self-custody as a fundamental right
- Minimal but predictable regulatory treatment focused on real economic activity rather than mere possession

## 6. Conclusion

Bitcoin is not a new type of investment security or commodity. It is a new form of money that exists alongside state currencies. Recognising it as an adjacent currency provides the most coherent framework for both taxation and regulation while respecting its technological reality.
