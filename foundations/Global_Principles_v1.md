# Global Principles for the Treatment of Bitcoin

**Version 1.0**  
**Published by MoloBTC**

## Core Principles

### 1. Bitcoin is Sui Generis

Bitcoin cannot be coherently classified under traditional legal categories of currency, capital, property, commodity, or security without creating fundamental contradictions. It is a thing of its own kind.

### 2. Bitcoin Functions as an Adjacent Currency

Bitcoin operates alongside state-issued currencies as a decentralised, borderless monetary instrument. It serves the core monetary functions of medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account.

### 3. Self-Custody is the Highest Form of Control

The ability to control Bitcoin through private keys or seed phrases known only to the owner represents the purest expression of property rights over Bitcoin. Self-custody should be protected as a fundamental right.

### 4. The Right to Noding

Operating a Bitcoin node is an exercise of free speech, freedom of association, and the right to participate in a decentralised monetary network. This right should be explicitly protected.

### 5. Taxation Should Target Realised Economic Activity

Mere holding or cross-border movement of self-custodied Bitcoin should not constitute a taxable event. Taxation should be limited to realised gains from voluntary economic activity with a sufficient territorial nexus.

### 6. Privacy of Mind and Cryptographic Control

Compelled disclosure of seed phrases or private keys constitutes compelled speech and an invasion of mental privacy. Such compulsion should be subject to the highest level of constitutional scrutiny.

### 7. Distinction Between Bitcoin and Other Crypto Assets

Bitcoin’s lack of issuer, fixed supply, and decentralised consensus distinguish it fundamentally from issuer-dependent crypto assets. Regulatory frameworks should maintain a clear separation.

### 8. Energy and Infrastructure Reality

Bitcoin’s proof-of-work mechanism creates real demand for energy and can incentivise the development of energy infrastructure. This characteristic has implications for energy policy and national infrastructure planning.

### 9. International Coordination Over Unilateral Control

Because Bitcoin is decentralised and global, effective policy requires international coordination rather than unilateral attempts at control. Attempts by individual states to assert comprehensive jurisdiction over self-custodied Bitcoin are practically unenforceable and conceptually flawed.

### 10. Withdrawal of Overreaching Regulation

Where existing or proposed regulations fail to respect Bitcoin’s sui generis nature, the most coherent policy response is withdrawal of such regulations or the creation of explicit carve-outs, rather than incremental adaptation of ill-fitting frameworks.
